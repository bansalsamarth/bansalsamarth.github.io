The Romantic Idiot

One year, endless misadventures—my unfiltered dating diary

By Samarth Bansal (25 min read)

22 July, 2023

I. The Reluctant

At the start of 2022, as a 28-year-old single man, I found myself in a peculiar position: the world wanted to solve a problem for me—a problem that did not exist.

Friends wanted to find me a girlfriend. Family wanted to find me a wife. I wanted neither.

“I don’t feel the need,” I told them. “It’s not like oxygen or water, right? You can live without a partner. Why should I have one? What’s the point?”

Many thought I was naive; some dismissed my questions as excuses; a few speculated about my sex life. But I was dead serious. I really didn’t get it: what’s the point?

I didn’t voice this thought aloud, but I couldn’t help pitying couples unable to chase their desires due to spousal constraints: dream jobs, spontaneous travel plans, flexible scheduling, undivided attention to personal goals.

It seemed foolish. Why do humans give up freedom so easily? Isn’t freedom the ultimate human quest?

Just one aspect stood against me in this internal dialogue. I had no experience. In my 28 years of existence, I had gone on exactly zero dates.

It’s not like my hormones were missing in action. I found great delight in watching reruns of cheesy romcoms and daydreaming about being the guy a girl would have at “hello”. Or having a female celebrity tell me she was just a girl, standing in front of a boy, asking me to love her. (Will I ever stop swooning over Julia Roberts? Highly doubt it.)

So, you see, I am not aromantic. It’s just that my romantic pursuits never move beyond unrequited crushes.

Five were serious: one in high school, two in college, two I met abroad. The last one was in 2018, during a journalism fellowship in the United States, which happened to be the only time I found the courage to walk up to a woman and ask her out. It ended in a cringe-worthy mess.

Visualise it. I was 25, finishing a three-week training program at the Missouri School of Journalism before heading off to work at the Wall Street Journal in New York. My crush, a fellow from Ukraine, would go on to work in a different city, so I thought I should reveal my feelings before parting ways.

After our last class, I asked her to walk with me to the supermarket. My nerves jangled as we set off. Struggling to find the words to confess my feelings, I stumbled through a tedious and monotonous build-up that seemed to drag on forever. I just couldn’t say it.

I rambled for at least twenty minutes until she cut me off mid-sentence with a piercingly blunt inquiry: “You want to say you like me, don’t you?”

When I finally mustered the courage to admit yes, she said no.

“You’re a great friend, Samarth, but...”

That’s me—a hopeless romantic with the dating experience of a naive teenager.

But as I progressed into my late twenties and learned more about myself, as I repeatedly asked myself what I truly wanted, as I made decisions to direct my life in the desired direction, the space for a partner vanished.

If I felt anything for anyone, I’d explain it away as some weird cocktail of pheromones, oxytocin, and dopamine casting its spell on my senses, pulling me away from what I truly desired. It was all about managing my brain chemistry—nothing else.

When people around me were getting into relationships with other people, I was working on building a relationship with myself.

I quit my full-time newspaper job, built a new life in the hills and pursued my creative and journalistic ambitions. I left my parent’s house to live on my own, learned to cook, manage household chores, handle finances, prioritise my health—and proudly started making overnight oats with berries, walnuts, fruits, and chia seeds to feel like a responsible adult.

So why disrupt what’s already working? Why add a chaotic human being to my life when I’m minimising the chaos in my life?

I worked through all possible scenarios in my diary, and singledom was always the answer. I felt intellectually sorted and emotionally at peace.

And then, life happened.

I found myself unwittingly drawn into the dating world. Don’t ask how because I don’t know. It just happened—shattering my delusions about the power of intellectual control.

I met someone. I dated. I was terrible at it. And then I couldn’t turn back. I found myself analysing potential partners, deciphering hidden meanings in DMs, and trying to tame my unruly hair. Discussing dating apps, relationship dynamics, and romantic experiences became second nature.

I behaved in ways I never thought I could, did things that made me hate myself, and spent months believing in my own fictions.

That was my last year. One hell of a year. I felt like an alien entering a new dimension of the adult world, doing things at 28 that most boys did at 18.

Objectively speaking, my dating adventures are driven by immaturity, overthinking, and a misguided sense of self-righteousness. They are marked by significant failures.

But they made me pause, think and reevaluate my perception of human relationships and life in general.

I’m not exaggerating.

Sometimes, seemingly banal events hold the greatest power to change us—moments where nothing really happens, yet everything happens. You are not materially changed, you are fundamentally changed. Something happens within us. Hidden truths are revealed, hidden fears become visible. Invisible to the worldly gaze, incomprehensible to our limited consciousness. But we know. We do.

That is the genesis of this essay. I am writing to make sense of that change.

II. The Clueless

My advisory council had a tough gig.

Their friend was more clueless than most people his age about the dating game, as if he had missed the memo that everyone else got about how to flirt, ask someone out, or even hold hands. It was on them to explain it all.

And I wanted to know it all. I aspired to be a “nice guy” and not a “fuckboi”. Someone who is not interested in playing the field or indulging in casual encounters, but is entering the world of dating to find something meaningful.

But when I first started seeing someone, I was paralysed. Like, I found this person attractive and interesting, which is why I asked her out, and I wanted to explore something, but I didn’t know what to do.

What was the process? What were the steps? How did you progress to the next stages? What were those stages? Could I be interested in someone else or not? What’s right and what’s wrong?

“Just get to know her more. Talk. Do things together,” the advisory council said. “You will figure out.”

So I did. Initially, it was thrilling. We chatted daily, asked about each other’s days, shared music, watched movies, and engaged in playful banter. Despite the long distance, we found a way to meet in person. It was great.

But it didn’t last long. I’m a terrible liar, and my actions revealed the truth. I wasn’t willing to sacrifice a day in my charming hill town, give up my reading time, lose an extra hour of sleep, or skip a workout to make time for dating.

Eventually, I didn’t look forward to our phone calls. They felt like a chore. I didn’t know what to discuss, and the excitement to share things faded. It all felt like an obligation because I was dating.

I was trying because I thought that’s what it was all about, but it was aimless trying. I had no idea what I was doing or why.

“You don’t feel like checking up once in a while?” she asked me, a few days before it was all over.

More questions followed.

“What do you want dude?”

“Can you picture yourself entwined with someone else?”

“Can you think of it in the next three years? Sharing your life with me, given everything works out?”

It took me a few weeks after parting ways to realise what was happening. I had no idea what it meant to share a life.

After spending years building a relationship with myself and growing accustomed to being alone, I had no clue how to make room for another person. The concept of having a partner—coexisting and navigating life together—was utterly terrifying. I was simply afraid of relationships, and I was incredibly immature when it came to being in one.

This realisation was far from comforting. It sucked. I wondered how other people figured it all out. (Or perhaps they didn’t? Maybe that’s one of the causes of unhappy relationships? As always, more questions than answers.)

But at least it revealed what I needed to work on if I desired a partner.

This fear was partly rooted in my childhood. Growing up in a dysfunctional family—where things fell apart at any moment for no apparent reason—deeply influenced my outlook on life.

I feared letting someone get close to me because another person meant inviting their problems. My emotional baggage made it impossible to imagine that a partner could bring joy and meaning. People equalled burdens. Nothing more.

I had known this for many years, but it was starkly visible in the dating context. The more I reflected, the more I realised how this baggage limited my potential for embracing the promise of relationships. I had to release this fear if I wanted to be with someone.

Because as I learned through conversations with my advisory council, dating and relationships demand intentionality. You have to envision your everyday life with a partner and how you fit into each other’s lives.

Beyond the initial euphoria and the fleeting butterflies, relationships requires an iterative process of trial and error. Partners need to actively explore shared values, interests, and goals, and make efforts to craft something meaningful.

While attraction makes the effort feel effortless, loving someone is not a passive occurrence in the long run. It is a choice that requires choosing to care for that person.

It hits you when it hits you—a lot has happened since. I now know better thanks to extensive journaling, supportive friends and observing couples who have crafted envy-worthy relationships. I was able to let go of my fears and reconsider the possibility that a relationship might be worth it.

I feel better, although I can’t help but ponder the gross injustice of one person’s education costing another person’s heart.

III. The Player

In the following months, I did everything possible to crack the code of the dating world. I start questioning my existence if I don’t understand things, so I had to.

My greatest challenge was finding interesting people. I couldn’t understand how others managed to find each other.

I must admit most of my struggles were self-inflicted. In April 2021, I moved to Landour, the serene sister town of Mussoorie that I now call home. You tell me: who in their twenties leaves Delhi to live a solitary life in the hills?

I unwittingly sabotaged my dating life by residing in a remote location for most of the month. I turned down party invites, skipped book club meetups, and gave up on random encounters at coffee shops or meet-cute at dance workshops.

I didn’t even have an office job that could introduce me to new faces. It was a conscious trade-off. I couldn’t complain about my life in Landour—still feels like a dream—but it did make dating an uphill task.

Like...

“The point is, you don’t stay here, which makes it tough,” a Delhi woman I was interested in told me via text. “I mean, I would have said, yeah, cool, let’s go on a date if you would have stayed here.”

I mulled over those two sentences for what felt like an eternity, sinking into the depths of “what if” scenarios that never fade away, forcing me to confront the consequences of my choices. So there was that.

Alongside, a series of revelations surfaced about the dating world in general.

The first revelation: I quickly discovered I was needlessly harsh on myself because I wasn’t the only clueless soul in town. Turned out that most singles around me were just as clueless—a bunch of amateurs just winging it.

Even those seemingly smug couples dispensing endless advice were no wiser. Many of them had never experienced the dating scene.

I mean…does India even have a dating culture?

Think about it. 95% of Indian marriages are arranged, so those couples are automatically excluded from the dating pool.

Many of the remaining lovebirds who choose their partners bypass the dating process. Some dive headfirst into relationships, some transition seamlessly from friends to lovers. The remaining—people like me—are crying on the internet.

Let’s make the distinction between dating and relationships clear: while dating involves exploration, getting to know people, assessing compatibility, and discovering shared interests, relationships elevate things to another level, with more commitment, greater expectations, and a higher place on the priority list.

They are not the same: you can be a fantastic partner but a lousy dater.

And approaching thirty brings its own set of challenges to the late-twenties dating scene. People my age have relatively more firm beliefs about love and life, making finding common ground trickier. It’s not as simple as growing together from early twenties.

So for sanity’s sake, I told myself: it’s okay to suck at dating. It’s okay to feel perpetually confused. It’s not like breathing that comes to us naturally. If anything, I should pat myself on the back for resisting conformity, fighting the marriage pressure, and daring to search for love.

The second revelation: Dating requires a whole bunch of skills. You heard me right—skills. Just like you can’t manage finances without knowing how to budget, save for emergencies, and invest for the future, stellar dating demands serious know-how.

Basic stuff: How to generate interest? How to express interest? How to handle rejection? How to communicate your needs and desires? How to open up?

The advisory council offered direction. Over a two-hour-long chat at a central Delhi cafe, over bruschetta and coffee, unspoken rules were revealed. Long list.

Take the Instagram DM game, for example. The women in the council shared that many women have interesting men on their list who are just “story friends”—they see them as some cool interesting guys on the internet, but nothing more. Don’t read too much into it.

To go beyond, learn how to talk. Avoid complimenting her looks since she’s likely bombarded with compliments already. Opt for teasing or playful banter to catch attention. Create an air of mystery. Don’t fit into a predictable box. Keep her intrigued. Don’t come across as needy. That’s a major turn-off.

Gosh. It all feels rather manipulative if you ask me. But apparently, everyone is following some version of this playbook, so you need to be in the know.

But knowing doesn’t help much. I spent endless hours seeking advice from the council, devouring YouTube videos, reading Reddit forums, and swiping Instagram reels. And it barely made a dent in my dating skills. Talking can only take you so far.

There is only one option: dive right in, fuck up, learn, fuck up a little less, learn a little more, and hope that one day you find what you are looking for.

I repeatedly made the mistake of crafting epically long and impatient messages to express interest—a real masterclass in silliness. It took me quite some time to grasp a simple truth: asking someone out is merely a proposition to get to know them better, not a marriage proposal. (And who doesn’t like to know they are wanted?)

All that’s needed is clear communication of intentions. Some version of: “I’m not desperate, I’m not needy; I’m genuinely interested in you, and I’d like to go out with you!” (Easier said than done, though.)

What often held me back was the fear of being perceived as creepy, intrusive, or inappropriate. Maybe upbringing conditioned me to think that way, or perhaps Bollywood influenced my perceptions. I’m not a mindless stalker, I know that, but movies often blur the lines in portraying romantic pursuits.

It’s a weird loop of confusion about the right and acceptable social norms, complicated by the fact that everyone has different comfort zones, making it impossible to establish a definitive rule.

It’s only when I took these risks—and, yes, got rejected, haha—that I realised expressing my interest respectfully and being mindful of boundaries turned out just fine. Perhaps I have a great taste, as the women who said no did so with grace, sparing me any awkward “why did I even tell them” moments.

Of course, if they weren’t interested, things did get a little awkward, but the fact that they didn’t block me and continued to talk was so reassuring. Maybe I had been overthinking it all along.

Getting there. But it’s a roller coaster.

The third revelation: Everyone knows this. Any social media app with a messaging feature effectively becomes a dating app. I know people who started dating through Twitter and Facebook. And I have heard it’s also happening on LinkedIn. (I can’t even.)

For me, that app was Instagram—and it sucked the life out of me.

When I fully embraced the identity of a dater, the advisory council urged me to put myself out there: “Use Instagram as a dating app. Show your holistic personality!”

Fair enough. How hard could it be? I just needed to adjust my online behaviour. Maybe I’m a four or a five—who knows!—but in the eyes of my crush(es), I had to project the image of a perfect ten.

So it began.

I posted stories to capture their attention. And painstakingly analysed their stories for the slightest hint of reciprocation. And if I found one, I lost all sense of composure, just like north Indian baratis on a dance floor when the DJ plays Sukhbir’s ’Oh Ho Ho Ho’.

Trust me, this covert mode of communication is being used all around. I’m not the only fool.

But all this drove me absolutely insane. Although I wasn’t lying or pretending to be someone I was not, I was putting on a show, and I despised myself for stooping so low. This wasn’t the real me.

On one of those dreadful days, I vented my frustration in my diary:

“I wasted twenty minutes after waking up at 4:30am in the morning to think about what to post on Instagram and how to sound smart and cool. And did not read the book I was planning to read in the morning. Fuck you, Samarth!”

Instagram really makes things complicated.

Just look at the never-ending array of signals that might mean something or might mean absolutely nothing: liking a post, commenting on a post, adding to close friends list, viewing a story, instantly viewing a story, not viewing a story, liking a story, replying to a story, replying with an emoji, replying with a comment, or the worse of the lot—leaving one on “seen”.

It’s as if the entire human experience is flattened to a limited number of digital actions and reactions, leading to new levels of awkwardness and manipulation. It’s pure hell.

I have now closed the Instagram dating chapter. No more story games. It’s not worth it. I am still an active user, and it’s more fun when I simply do what I feel like doing there. (Posting photos, what else?)

The fourth revelation: Men often find themselves portrayed as the culprits of the toxic millennial dating culture. There’s some truth to that—I’m not excusing myself here.

Traditional gender roles impose rigid expectations on both men and women, reinforcing stereotypes. You don’t need expertise in feminist literature to know women bear the brunt of this burden; bare minimum societal awareness is enough.

The problem lies in the polarisation of this much-needed discussion. It fails to acknowledge the struggles many men face. Sometimes, we feel lost, unsure of the right moves to make, and sometimes, we are downright terrified.

Yes, patriarchy has taught us to suppress our vulnerable sides, but I can assure you we still have them.

Talk about looks. Being attractive is one of the biggest privileges someone can have in the dating world, and it’s just plain weird to pretend otherwise. (Some do.)

I’ve always been aware of this fact, but it truly hit me a couple years ago, when I underwent a significant weight loss during the pandemic. The first time I left home after shedding those extra kilos, I found myself on the receiving end of flirtation for the first time.

I was lost in my writing, headphones on, when she greeted me and said, “I hope you didn’t overhear my phone conversation with my friend.”

I reassured her, pointing to my headphones. “Ah, great,” she said. “I was just asking her where have all the single men in Delhi gone?”

I knew where this was headed. She gave herself permission to share her struggles with her ex and then asked if she could join me at my table—a request I couldn’t refuse. I was in the middle of writing a long Mint explainer on data privacy, and the deadline was haunting me, but I was also enjoying the attention, so I let it be.

How could I not? She was lavishing me with flattering comments about how young I looked (she guessed my age to be five years younger than I actually was). We chatted for a while, and she left her phone number with me.

Damn. I had never experienced anything like this before, and it made me think, “This is what attractive men go through?”

What changed? Nothing. Except I was now wearing S-sized shirts. It’s as if somehow, overnight, I became visible to the world.

Just to clarify, I am not ranting, nor am I claiming to be good-looking—lol, I don’t even meet the low bar of being passably hot—and neither am I exempting myself from the physical attraction bias. Quite the opposite, in fact.

Any data scientist at Tinder, Bumble, or Hinge could use my swiping patterns and expose my slavery to conventional—and admittedly problematic—beauty standards.

Now, look, I am a reader. I know our desires are heavily influenced by societal norms that aren’t equitable for everyone. However, this knowledge doesn’t automatically change my romantic preferences overnight. It’s a confusing struggle within myself, involving how I see myself and how I see others.

Dating apps just magnify these struggles to a whole new level. They transform us into virtual commodities, stripping away our differences and dignity. Swipe, swipe, swipe, and hope to strike gold.

And know what? For most of us men, it’s like searching for dust. Zero matches. Nil. Nada. It’s embarrassing and makes us question our self-worth.

On good days, after hours of swiping for two or three days, I might get two or three matches, and even then, it’s often unfulfilling. It feels inhumane.

And the questions keep haunting me:

Is the algorithm flawed?

Or my profile?

Is my first photograph off?

Or am I the one who’s off?

Hinge, in particular, creeps me out by showing profiles of people I know: a former crush (and she liked me), an ongoing crush, a cousin, the attractive ex of a good friend.

The weirdest thing happened when I sent a ‘rose’ to a woman—you can send one ‘rose’ a week to convey special interest (so cringey). We didn’t match. Which is fine. But then, a few days later, I received an email from her inviting me to speak on a panel for the flagship conference of one of Delhi’s premier think tanks where she works. I wondered if she knew. Nuts.

At this point, I must reveal I did experience a rare stroke of luck when I met someone amazing through Hinge. She had a prompt referencing Fleabag’s horrific “It’ll pass”scene, on which I obviously have thoughts, sparking a long conversation that eventually led us to go out for a while. (Thanks you, Phoebe Waller-Bridge!)

Unfortunately, it didn’t work out, but it stood out as a glimmer of hope in the midst of overwhelming frustrations through the swiping process. Just too much to handle.

Consequently, a familiar pattern emerged: I’d uninstall the apps, swearing never to use them again, only to find myself downloading them a month later—a never-ending cycle of digital torture and self-inflicted madness.

So yeah, many of us men find ourselves caught in the middle: not quite the Shahrukhs of the world, but also not the incels or MRAs on the other extreme. We are just normal people trying to find our way through a needlessly complex landscape.

An anonymous user on Reddit summed it up brilliantly:

For men, dating is like dying of thirst in the desert. Not a drop of water in sight. For women, it’s like dying of thirst in the ocean. Water is everywhere, but it’s mostly toxic and full of salt.”

Ask anyone. Dating is undeniably exhausting—provably suboptimal—a relentless cycle of meeting new people, online and offline, trying and failing, oscillating between bouts of confidence and self-doubt, judging others and getting ourselves judged, navigating the mess that comes uninvited with the Homo Sapiens identity.

It often feels like an unpaid full-time job, draining one emotionally and mentally. That’s the reality. Period.

If I know this, why am I willingly subject myself to this lunacy? Has the Instagram algorithm not shown me enough self-love self-care reels?

Why?

The answer I discovered changed the way I think.

IV. The Dreamer

All my memories of myself revolve around my insatiable curiosity about the world. While I can’t claim the title of the ultimate nerd (how I wish I could!) I definitely rank somewhere high up in the hierarchy.

In my mind, everything around us—including the most mysterious questions about our existence—can be explained through dispassionate observation and rigorous thought. Science first. Life may appear complicated, but when you strip it down to its core, it’s just atoms doing their thing.

That’s my IIT brain at work: reason over emotion, analysis over intuition, logic over magic.

But, oh boy, a year spent immersed in the dating world proved me wrong.

Let me tell you a story.

“You like her, don’t you?” the advisory council asked me one windy evening at a rustic heritage restaurant in Landour. “Just listen to how you talk about her,” they said.

I didn’t want to admit that I liked her. Something held me back—perhaps fear. Because guys like me don’t expect attention from girls like her.

Look at her and look at me: she’s smart, funny, quirky, and incredibly gorgeous. She sneezes like a baby elephant and somehow looks cute doing it. Her wild curly hair refuses to behave, but it only adds to her charm. And she’s got pretty feet (she said it, not me).

On Instagram, where we met, she effortlessly stands out. Her reels got to me: when she twirls around in a saree wearing her infectious smile, Beethoven symphony playing in the background, it’s like watching a movie star. La la land.

Then there’s me: shabby hair, a fuzzy face, perpetually wrinkled shirts, and the audacity to wear comfy H&M shorts on a date (don’t ask), all squeezed into a majestic 5’5" frame.

But wait. It’s not like I’m crushing on every pretty girl I encounter—I don’t easily get attracted. So yes, her face caught my attention, but what drew me in was the alluring reflection of her inner life.

She’s a nature lover through and through: mountains, skies, stars, flowers—the whole shebang, the type of person who stops in the middle of a busy street to take a picture of a flower growing out of a crack in the sidewalk. She could have been a modern-day Thoreau, except that she got stuck in the unfortunate world of management consulting.

She draws and clicks and writes. Her work spoke to me, an unspoken hard-to-describe connection only art can establish. Words come to life when she puts thoughts on paper which she does not often do.

Her raw and revealing blog features poetry exploring questions about human existence, prose that marvels at nature’s vivid colours, and meditations on love and life—and damn, she does not waste syllables. She daringly shares her struggles with feelings of emptiness and sometimes, a sense of being lost, expressed through an endless stream-of-consciousness questions, like in the pages of my own diary.

Everything I saw collectively revealed how she is an observer of the outside and the inside world, a quality which needs a special kind of intelligence, the kind that reveals something beautiful about the way she experiences the world, the kind I find incredibly attractive.

This version of her existed in my head—the description of a “random stranger” I shared with the advisory council at the Landour dinner, an idealised image fueled by online intel. (Hi there, an investigative journalist here!)

All this without even having met her. I don’t know how I fell for her. All I know is I did. It just felt…beautiful. Something out of a dream.

If I were to channel my inner Mr. Darcy from a Jane Austen novel, I’d say, “I was in the middle before I knew that I had begun.”

How did this even happen? Am I a complete idiot?

Do I not understand that our online presence is a curated version of ourselves? That it does not fully represent who we really are? That whatever online interaction I had with her didn’t mean much? What if she turned out to be a serial killer, or the one who learns “career hacks” from Hitler, or an unabashed fan of Tony Kakkar? What will I do then? Cry at Goa Beach?

I know. I know it very well. Yet, again, none of that knowledge helped.

Logic failed me.

I just can’t understand the mechanics of attraction: it does not seem to follow any rules, there is no pattern, and it is definitely not predictable.

And then, boom. In a reality-defying coincidence, we had a brief flirtatious exchange on the very dinner I told the advisory council she exists in my head. Dopamine levels were off the charts because in that moment, I just felt the attraction might be mutual.

It wasn’t.

Weeks later, when I found the courage to tell her I like her, she responded with a long, thoughtful reply to my stupidly long message.

The only problem was, her response didn’t provide a definitive answer.

It took her two hours to say what seemed like, “I don’t know”, but it took me two months to know that “I don’t know“ is often a polite way to say “no”.

This meant confusion. Which bred anxiety. I kept telling myself it was just a matter of time. She will get it. But it was more like the “It’s me, hi, I’m the problem it’s me” scenario—I didn’t get it.

So there I was, confronting the harsh reality of one-sided attraction, which, putting it mildly, is not the greatest feeling. Ughhh.

I found myself grappling with questions: Was her unavailability due to genuine busyness, or was she simply not interested? Her monosyllabic responses should have been a clear indication of disinterest, yet I persisted. At times, I worried about coming across as pushy, and at other times, I felt inadequate, wondering if I wasn’t doing enough.

The advisory council demanded I must never appear desperate, which meant I had to fake it, because, for lord’s sake, at one point, I was desperate. I can confirm it is absolutely possible to miss someone even if you have never met.

And that’s when I encountered the strange and parasitic side of what we call hope.

Hope held on tightly when my brain clearly saw no point. Hope overtook analytical thinking. Hope amplified the faintest signs of potential interest and ignored the glaring signs of disinterest.

Logic failed me. Again.

So when I lost hope, it felt like a deep void—to feel I had lost something even when I never had it. Which is the crazy thing because nothing had really happened. I am sorry, I just had you read hundreds of words to tell a six-word story: I was interested, she was not. The end.

The hard part wasn’t the rejection. The hard part was acceptance. The painful truth that my feelings wouldn’t be reciprocated. That we couldn’t waltz beneath a starlit sky or share a popcorn tub at the cinema. I filled the pages of my diary, played James Blunt on repeat, and sought art that made me feel seen.

Whatever this feeling was, it just lingered. I didn’t know what to do with it. Some offered banal advice like “move on, there are so many fish in the sea!” Idiots. Was I out shopping for headphones, where I could easily swap one for another? It’s not that simple. I just couldn’t bring myself to create an account on dating apps or show interest in someone else.

I have heard tales of tragic love, but mine doesn’t even qualify as a sob story—it has no epic narrative. And I have to confront it when the story-less story is subjected to opinions of others: they dismiss it as mere infatuation, a reflection of unrealistic expectations fueled by mindless romcoms and plain immaturity.

Maybe that’s your take too. And maybe you’re right.

But the thing is, I don’t care anymore. I genuinely don’t. I’m done overusing my left brain to intellectualise my feelings. And I refuse to let anyone invalidate their authenticity. They belong to me alone, and I know they were as real as death and taxes.

My dating adventures taught me a life lesson I didn’t learn any other way: there are limitations to our understanding. Some things are beyond clear comprehension. They are better experienced than dissected and analysed. The nerd in me must let go the desire for explanations, and allow some things to be as they are.

Whatever this mysterious force is, it’s what keeps me hanging on in this comically brutal world of dating.

Logic be damned.

V. The Believer

As I began writing this essay, I had an epiphany—the futility of comparing the lives of single and coupled individuals, a predominantly heteronormative discourse, a narrative I’ve unwittingly been part of.

To see through this sham, I realised it’s crucial to reject the notion that there is a single “right” way to structure a fulfilling life. There isn’t one.

I am privileged enough to have the agency to choose what feels right for me. I can embrace the joys of singlehood or share my life with someone I genuinely connect with. I can’t let societal expectations dictate such an intimate part of my life.

This became crystal clear once I figured that those who advocate the “right” way often do so to justify their choices and find psychological comfort.

Which does not matter. Especially for individuals like me—who are financially independent, value personal autonomy, and find societal norms suffocating. My happiness and fulfilment are independent of my relationship status.

For now, I am choosing to believe in the story that a romantic relationship has the potential to make life a little more meaningful.

For now, I am choosing to believe that finding love is worth it. I’m still scared as hell of making mistakes and getting hurt. But I guess it’s part of the deal: I can’t possibly get there without accepting that my inexperienced heart might get hurt again.

For now, I am letting my guard down and letting things happen. Let’s see.

Because after an exhausting year, I realised it’s all about taking chances. And having faith. I can’t be fully sure if I’m choosing the right person or if there’s someone better out there. I can’t guarantee eternal love. I can’t predict what the future holds. The fear is real.

But as the Hot Priest wisely said in Fleabag:

So for now, I am choosing to believe in something wonderful. Who knows anything.

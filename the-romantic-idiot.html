The Romantic Idiot

One year, endless misadventures. My unfiltered dating diary.

By Samarth Bansal (25 min read)

22 July, 2023

I. The Reluctant

At the start of 2022, as a 28-year-old single man, I found myself in a peculiar position: the world wanted to solve a problem for me—a problem that did not exist.

Friends wanted to find me a girlfriend. Family wanted to find me a wife. I wanted neither.

“I don’t feel the need for a partner,” I told my friends. “It’s not like oxygen or water, right? You can live without a partner. Why should I have one? What’s the point?”

Many thought I was naive; some dismissed my questions as excuses; a few wondered about my sex life. But I was dead serious. I really didn’t get it: what’s the point?

I didn’t voice this thought aloud, but I felt pity for couples who, even momentarily, lamented their inability to pursue their desires because of spousal constraints: dream jobs, spontaneous travel plans, flexible scheduling, undivided attention to personal goals.

It seemed foolish to me. Why would anyone give up their freedoms so easily? Isn’t freedom the ultimate human quest?

Just one aspect stood against me in this internal debate. I had no experience. In my 28 years of existence, I had gone on exactly zero dates.

It’s not like my hormones were missing in action. I found great delight in watching reruns of cheesy romcoms and daydreaming about being the guy a girl would have at “hello”. Or having a female celebrity tell me she was just a girl, standing in front of a boy, asking me to love her. (Will I ever stop swooning over Julia Roberts? Highly doubt it.)

So, you see, I am not aromantic. It’s just that my romantic pursuits never move beyond unrequited crushes.

Five were serious: one in high school, two in college, two I met abroad. The last one was in 2018, during a journalism fellowship in the United States, which happened to be the only time I found the courage to walk up to a woman and ask her out. It ended in a cringe-worthy mess.

Visualise it. I was 25, finishing a three-week training program at the Missouri School of Journalism before heading off to work at the Wall Street Journal in New York. My crush, a fellow from Ukraine, would go on to work in a different city, so I thought I should reveal my feelings before parting ways.

After our last class, I asked her to walk with me to the supermarket. My nerves jangled as we set off. Struggling to find the words to confess my feelings, I stumbled through a tedious and monotonous build-up that seemed to drag on forever. I just couldn’t say it.

I rambled for at least twenty minutes until she cut me off mid-sentence with a piercingly blunt inquiry: “You want to say you like me, don’t you?”

When I finally mustered the courage to admit yes, she said no.

“You’re a great friend, Samarth, but...”

That’s me—a hopeless romantic with the dating experience of a clueless teenager.

But as I progressed into my late twenties and learned more about myself, as I repeatedly asked myself what I truly wanted, as I made decisions to direct my life in the desired direction, the space for a partner vanished.

If I felt anything for anyone, I’d explain it away as some weird cocktail of pheromones, oxytocin, and dopamine casting its spell on my senses, pulling me away from what I truly desired. It was all about managing my brain chemistry—nothing else.

When people around me were getting into relationships with other people, I was working on building a relationship with myself.

I quit my full-time newspaper job, built a new life in the hills and pursued my creative and journalistic ambitions. I left my parent’s house to live on my own, learned to cook, manage household chores, handle finances, prioritise my health—and began making overnight oats with berries, walnuts, fruits, and chia seeds to feel like a responsible adult.

So why disrupt what’s already working? Why add a chaotic human being to my life when I’m minimising the chaos in my life?

I worked through all possible scenarios in my diary, and singledom was always the answer. I felt intellectually sorted and emotionally at peace.

And then, life happened.

I found myself unwittingly drawn into the dating world. Don’t ask how because I don’t know. It just happened—shattering my delusions about the power of intellectual control.

I met someone. I dated. I was terrible at it. And then I couldn’t turn back. I found myself analysing potential partners, deciphering hidden meanings in DMs, and trying to tame my unruly hair. Discussing dating apps, relationship dynamics, and romantic experiences became second nature.

I behaved in ways I never thought I could, did things that made me hate myself, and spent months believing in my own fictions.

That was my last year. One hell of a year. I felt like an alien entering a new dimension of the adult world, doing things at 28 that most boys did at 18.

Objectively speaking, my dating adventures are driven by immaturity, overthinking, and a misguided sense of self-righteousness. They are marked by significant failures.

But they made me pause, think and reevaluate my perception of human relationships and life in general.

I’m not exaggerating.

Sometimes, seemingly banal events hold the greatest power to change us—moments where nothing really happens, yet everything happens. You are not materially changed, you are fundamentally changed. Something happens within us. Hidden truths are revealed, hidden fears become visible. Invisible to the worldly gaze, incomprehensible to our limited consciousness. But we know. We do.

That is the genesis of this essay. I am writing this to make sense of that change.

II. The Clueless

My advisory council had a tough gig.

Their friend was more clueless than most people his age about the dating game, as if he had missed the memo that everyone else got about how to flirt, ask someone out, or even hold hands. It was on them to explain it all.

And I wanted to know it all. I aspired to be a “nice guy” and not a “fuckboi”. Someone who is not interested in playing the field or indulging in casual encounters, but is entering the world of dating to find something meaningful.

But when I first started seeing someone, I was paralysed. Like, I found this person attractive and interesting, which is why I asked her out, and I wanted to explore something, but I didn’t know what to do.

What was the process? What were the steps? How did you progress to the next stages? What were those stages? Could I be interested in someone else or not? What’s right and what’s wrong?

“Just get to know her more. Talk. Do things together,” the advisory council said. “You will figure out.”

So I did. Initially, it was thrilling. We chatted daily, asked about each other’s days, shared music, watched movies, and engaged in playful banter. Despite the long distance, we found a way to meet in person. It was great.

But it didn’t last long. I’m a terrible liar, and my actions revealed the truth. I wasn’t willing to sacrifice a day in my charming hill town, give up my reading time, lose an extra hour of sleep, or skip a workout to make time for dating.

Eventually, I didn’t look forward to our phone calls. They felt like a chore. I didn’t know what to discuss, and the excitement to share things faded. It all felt like an obligation because I was dating.

I was trying because I thought that’s what it was all about, but it was aimless trying. I had no idea what I was doing or why.

“You don’t feel like checking up once in a while?” she asked me, a few days before it was all over.

More questions followed.

“What do you want dude?”

“Can you picture yourself entwined with someone else?”

“Can you think of it in the next three years? Sharing your life with me, given everything works out?”

It took me a few weeks after parting ways to realise what was happening. I had no idea what it meant to share a life.

After spending years building a relationship with myself and growing accustomed to being alone, I had no clue how to make room for another person. The concept of having a partner—coexisting and navigating life together—was utterly terrifying. I was simply afraid of relationships, and I was incredibly immature when it came to being in one.

This realisation was far from comforting. It sucked. I wondered how other people figured it all out. (Or perhaps they didn’t? Maybe that’s one of the causes of unhappy relationships? As always, more questions than answers.)

But at least it revealed what I needed to work on if I desired a partner.

This fear was partly rooted in my childhood. Growing up in a dysfunctional family—where things fell apart at any moment for no apparent reason—deeply influenced my outlook on life.

I feared letting someone get close to me because another person meant inviting their problems. My emotional baggage made it impossible to imagine that a partner could bring joy and meaning. People equalled burdens. Nothing more.

I had known this for many years, but it was starkly visible in the dating context. The more I reflected, the more I realised how this baggage limited my potential for embracing the promise of relationships. I had to release this fear if I wanted to be with someone.

Because as I learned through conversations with my advisory council, dating and relationships demand intentionality. You have to envision your everyday life with a partner and how you fit into each other’s lives.

Beyond the initial euphoria and the fleeting butterflies, relationships requires an iterative process of trial and error. Partners need to actively explore shared values, interests, and goals, and make efforts to craft something meaningful. While attraction may make the effort feel effortless, loving someone is not a passive occurrence in the long run. It is a choice that requires choosing to care for that person.

It hits you when it hits you—a lot has happened since. I now know better thanks to extensive journaling, supportive friends and observing couples who have crafted envy-worthy relationships. I was able to let go of my fears and reconsider the possibility that a relationship might be worth it.

I feel better, although I can’t help but ponder the gross injustice of one person’s education costing another person’s heart.

III. The Player

In the following months, I did everything possible to crack the code of the dating world. I start questioning my existence if I don’t understand things, so I had to.

My greatest challenge was finding interesting people. I couldn’t understand how others managed to find each other.

I must admit most of my struggles were self-inflicted. In April 2021, I moved to Landour, the serene sister town of Mussoorie that I now call home. You tell me: who in their twenties leaves Delhi to live a solitary life in the hills?

I unwittingly sabotaged my dating life by residing in a remote location for most of the month. I turned down party invites, skipped book club meetups, and gave up on random encounters at coffee shops or meet-cute at dance workshops.

I didn’t even have an office job that could introduce me to new faces. It was a conscious trade-off. I couldn’t complain about my life in Landour—still feels like a dream—but it did make dating an uphill task.

Like...

“The point is, you don’t stay here, which makes it tough,” a Delhi woman I was interested in told me via text. “I mean, I would have said, yeah, cool, let’s go on a date if you would have stayed here.”

I mulled over those two sentences for what felt like an eternity, sinking into the depths of “what if” scenarios that never fade away, forcing me to confront the consequences of my choices. So there was that.

Alongside, a series of revelations surfaced about the dating world in general.

The first revelation: I quickly discovered I was needlessly harsh on myself because I wasn’t the only clueless soul in town. Turned out that most singles around me were just as clueless—a bunch of amateurs just winging it.

Even those seemingly smug couples dispensing endless advice were no wiser. Many of them had never experienced the dating scene.

I mean…does India even have a dating culture?

Think about it. 95% of Indian marriages are arranged, so those couples are automatically excluded from the dating pool.

Many of the remaining lovebirds who choose their partners bypass the dating process. Some dive headfirst into relationships, some transition seamlessly from friends to lovers. The remaining—people like me—are crying on the internet.

Because look, dating is about exploration: getting to know people, assessing compatibility, and discovering shared interests. It doesn’t necessarily imply exclusivity. Relationships, on the other hand, elevate things to another level. They come with more commitment, greater expectations, and a higher place on the priority list.

The two are entirely different ball games: you can be a fantastic partner but a lousy dater.

And approaching thirty brings its own set of challenges to the late-twenties dating scene. People my age have relatively more firm beliefs about love and life, making finding common ground trickier than ever. It’s not as simple as growing together from early twenties.

So here’s the thing I told myself: it’s perfectly okay to suck at dating. It’s okay to feel perpetually confused. It’s not something that comes naturally to a human being, like breathing.

If anything, I should pat myself on the back for resisting conformity, fighting the marriage pressure, and daring to search for love.

The second revelation: Dating requires a whole bunch of skills. You heard me right—skills. Just like you can’t manage finances without knowing how to budget, save for emergencies, and invest for the future, being a stellar dater demands some serious know-how.

It’s hard to succeed without grasping the basics. How do you generate interest? (Feels like a damn sales job!) How do you express your interest? How do you handle rejection? How do you communicate your needs and desires? How do you open up and establish trust?

The advisory council provided direction. Over a two-hour-long chat at a central Delhi cafe, over bruschetta and coffee, I was enlightened about the unspoken rules.

Take the DM game, for example: don’t compliment a woman on her looks because they are bombarded with compliments already. Opt for teasing or playful banter to catch her attention. Create an air of mystery. Don’t fit into a predictable box. Keep her intrigued. Avoid coming across as needy—that’s a major turn-off. Find the right balance.

It all feels rather manipulative if you ask me. But apparently, everyone is following some version of this playbook, so you need to be in the know.

But knowing doesn’t help much. I spent endless hours seeking advice from the council, devouring YouTube videos, reading Reddit forums, and swiping Instagram reels. And it barely made a dent in my dating skills. Talking can only take you so far.

There is only one option: dive right in, fuck up, learn, fuck up a little less, learn a little more, and hope that one day you find what you are looking for.

Dating is undeniably exhausting. It’s provably suboptimal: a relentless cycle of meeting new people, online and offline, like an unpaid full-time job that drains you emotionally and mentally. That’s the reality. Period.

The third revelation: Everyone knows this. Any social media app with a messaging feature effectively becomes a dating app. I know people who started dating through Twitter and Facebook. And I have heard it’s also happening on LinkedIn. (I can’t even.)

For me, that app was Instagram—and it sucked the life out of me.

When I fully embraced the identity of a dater, the advisory council urged me to put myself out there: “Use Instagram as a dating app. Show your holistic personality!”

Fair enough. How hard could it be? I just needed to adjust my online behaviour. Because I wasn’t meeting many people in person. Maybe I’m a four or a five—who knows!—but in the eyes of my crush(es), I had to project the image of a perfect ten.

So it began.

I posted stories to capture their attention. And painstakingly analysed their stories for the slightest hint of reciprocation. And if I found one, I lost all sense of composure, just like north Indian baratis on a dance floor when the DJ plays Sukhbir’s ’Oh Ho Ho Ho’.

Trust me, this covert mode of communication is being used all around. I’m not the only fool.

But all this messes with your head. The constant posting and interpretation drove me absolutely insane.

Although I wasn’t lying or pretending to be someone I was not, I was putting on a show, and I despised myself for stooping so low. This wasn’t the real me.

On one of those dreadful days, I vented my frustration in my diary:

“I wasted twenty minutes after waking up at 4:30am in the morning to think about what to post on Instagram and how to sound smart and cool. And did not read the book I was planning to read in the morning. Fuck you, Samarth!”

Instagram makes everything complicated.

Just look at the never-ending array of signals that might mean something or might mean absolutely nothing: liking a post, commenting on a post, viewing a story, instantly viewing a story, not viewing a story, liking a story, replying to a story, replying with an emoji, replying with a comment, not replying to a comment, leaving it on “seen”.

It’s as if the entire human experience has been flattened to a limited number of digital actions and reactions, leading to new levels of awkwardness and manipulation. It’s pure hell.

I have now closed the Instagram dating chapter—no more story games. It’s not worth it. I am still an active user, and it’s more fun when I simply do what I feel like doing there.

The fourth revelation: Much of the millennial dating discourse paints men as the culprits of a toxic dating culture. And yes, there’s some truth to that—I’m not excusing myself here.

Traditional gender roles impose rigid expectations on both men and women, reinforcing stereotypes that limit individual expression. You don’t need to be an expert in feminist literature to understand that women bear the brunt of this burden. Bare minimum societal awareness is enough.

However, the problem with this polarised narrative is the failure to acknowledge the genuine struggles many men face. Sometimes we feel lost, unsure of the right moves to make, and sometimes we are downright terrified. Yes, patriarchy has taught us to suppress our vulnerable sides, but I can assure you that we still have them.

Talk about looks. Being attractive is one of the biggest privileges someone can have in the dating world, and it’s just plain weird to pretend otherwise. (Some do.)

I’ve always been aware of this fact, but it truly hit me a couple years ago, when I underwent a significant weight loss during the pandemic. The first time I ventured out after the lockdown eased and shed those extra kilos, a girl flirted with me at the café where I was working.

I was lost in my writing, headphones on, when she greeted me and said, “I hope you didn’t overhear my conversation with my friend.” I reassured her, pointing to my headphones, but she proceeded to inquire, “Where have all the single men in Delhi gone?”

I knew where this was headed. She shared her struggles with her ex and politely asked if she could join me at my table, all while lavishing me with flattering comments about how young I looked (she guessed my age to be five years younger than I actually was). We chatted for a while, and she left her phone number with me.

Damn. I had never experienced anything like this before, and it made me think, “So, this is what attractive men go through?”

What changed? Nothing. Except for my appearance. It’s as if somehow, overnight, I became visible to the world.

Just to clarify, I am not ranting, nor am I claiming to be good-looking—lol, I don’t even meet the low bar of being passably hot—and neither am I exempting myself from the physical attraction bias. Quite the opposite, in fact.

Any data scientist at Tinder, Bumble, or Hinge could expose based on my swiping patterns that I’m a slave to conventional—and admittedly problematic—“beauty” standards.

I know that our desires are heavily influenced by societal norms that aren’t equitable for everyone, but this understanding doesn’t automatically enable me to change my romantic preferences overnight.

It’s a confusing struggle within myself, involving how I see myself and how I see others.

And dating apps just magnify these struggles to a whole new level. They transform us into virtual commodities, stripping away our differences and dignity. Swipe, swipe, swipe, and hope to strike gold.

For most of us men, it’s like searching for dust. Zero matches. Nil. Nada. It’s embarrassing and makes us question our self-worth.

On good days, after hours of swiping for two or three days, I might get two or three matches, and even then, it’s often unfulfilling. It feels inhumane.

And the questions keep haunting me: Is the algorithm flawed? Or my profile? Is my first photograph off? Or am I the one who’s off?

Hinge, in particular, has a knack for creeping me out by showing profiles of people I know, including a former crush (who sent me a “like”), an ongoing crush, a cousin, and even the very attractive ex of a good friend.

The weirdest thing happened when I sent a ‘rose’ to a woman—you only get to send one ‘rose’ a week to convey special interest in someone (so cringey). We didn’t match. Which is fine. But then, a few days later, I received an email from her inviting me to speak on a panel for the flagship conference of one of Delhi’s premier think tanks where she works. I wondered if she knew. Nuts.

At this point, I must reveal I did experience a rare stroke of luck when I met someone amazing through Hinge. She had a prompt referencing Fleabag’s horrific “It’ll pass”scene, on which I have deep thoughts, which sparked a long conversation, eventually leading us to go out for a while.

Unfortunately, it didn’t work out, but it stood out as a glimmer of hope in the midst of my overwhelming frustrations through the swiping process—just too much to handle.

So it became a routine: I’d uninstall the apps, swearing never to use them again, only to find myself downloading them a month later—a never-ending cycle of digital torture and self-inflicted madness.

So yeah, many of us men find ourselves caught in the middle—not quite the Shahrukhs of the world, but also not the incels or MRAs on the other extreme. We are just normal people trying to find our way through a needlessly complex landscape.

An anonymous user on Reddit summed it up brilliantly:

For men, dating is like dying of thirst in the desert. Not a drop of water in sight. For women, it’s like dying of thirst in the ocean. Water is everywhere, but it’s mostly toxic and full of salt.”

Despite all this, I think I’ve actually become better at this whole thing—still lots to learn, far from perfect, but there’s been a shift.

For instance, asking someone out felt nerve-wracking. I couldn’t do it without the push from the advisory council. And even then, I’d made the mistake of writing epically long and impatient messages—a real masterclass in silliness.

But it has gotten easier. Relatively. It starts with having confidence in myself, expressing my interest respectfully, and being mindful of boundaries. All I need to communicate is: I’m not desperate, I’m not needy, I’m just genuinely interested in you, and I’d like to go out with you!

I’ve also become quite the connoisseur of rejections—a recurring theme in my dating life, haha. But I’ve learned not to take it personally and realise that rejection often does not reflect my flaws. It’s often a result of people’s personal circumstances and preferences—desire is a deeply personal thing. Hats off to all those women who gracefully delivered their “no” with a touch of class.

With each new experience, I learn new things about how to navigate the dating world and what not to do. There is, of course, collateral damage—missed chances due to my own screw-ups—but perhaps that’s an inevitable part of the process.

Getting there. But it’s a roller coaster.

IV. The Dreamer

All my memories of myself revolve around my insatiable curiosity about the world. While I can’t claim the title of the ultimate nerd (how I wish I could!) I definitely rank somewhere high up in the hierarchy.

In my mind, everything around us—including the most mysterious questions about our existence—can be explained through dispassionate observation and rigorous thought. Life may appear complicated, but when you strip it down to its core, it’s just atoms doing their thing.

That’s how my IIT brain generally works. It prioritises reason over emotion, analysis over intuition, logic over magic. It seeks clarity and truth to navigate the chaos of modernity.

But little did I know that a year spent immersed in the dating world would prove just how mistaken I was.

Let me tell you a story.

“You like her, don’t you?” the advisory council asked me one windy evening at a rustic heritage restaurant in Landour. “Just listen to how you talk about her,” they said.

I didn’t want to admit that I liked her. Something held me back—perhaps fear. Because guys like me don’t expect attention from girls like her.

Look at her and look at me: she’s smart, funny, quirky, and incredibly gorgeous. She sneezes like a baby elephant and somehow looks cute doing it. Her wild curly hair refuses to behave, but it only adds to her charm. And she’s got pretty feet (she said it, not me).

On Instagram, where we met, she effortlessly stands out. Her reels got to me: as she twirls around in a saree with her infectious smile with Beethoven symphony playing in the background, it’s like watching a movie star. La la land.

Then there’s me: shabby hair, a fuzzy face, perpetually wrinkled shirts, and the audacity to wear comfy H&M shorts on a date (don’t ask)—all squeezed into a majestic 5’5" frame.

But it’s not like I’m crushing on every pretty girl I encounter. Far from it. There’s more to her—her inner world. That’s what drew me in.

She’s a nature lover through and through—mountains, skies, stars, flowers—the whole shebang, the type of person who stops in the middle of a busy street just to take a picture of a flower growing out of a crack in the sidewalk. She could have been a modern-day Thoreau, except that she got stuck in the unfortunate world of management consulting.

Her words come to life when she writes, which she does not often do. Reading her (now outdated) blog felt like diving into her diary. It’s raw and revealing. She shared her struggles with feelings of emptiness and, sometimes, a sense of being lost, hinting that she’s actively searching for her place in the world.

If life is the sum total of things we pay attention to, her online presence reflects her love for beauty in everyday things. Not the shallow “glamour” our culture has distorted, but the beauty found in small moments, like the joy derived from a great song or the contemplation of a painting at a museum.

Everything I saw collectively revealed how she is an observer of the outside and the inside world, a quality which needs a special kind of intelligence, the kind that reveals something beautiful about the way she experiences the world, the kind I find incredibly attractive.

This version of her existed in my head—the description of a “random stranger” I shared with the advisory council during the Landour dinner, fueled by online intel and superior stalking skills. (Hi there, an investigative journalist here!)

I didn’t have any checklist where she ticked the boxes. And while I have tried to rationalise my reasons for attraction, I don’t think they do justice—it’s hard for me to pinpoint exactly why.

It just felt…beautiful. Something out of a dream.

If I were to channel my inner Mr. Darcy from a Jane Austen novel, I’d say, “I was in the middle before I knew that I had begun.”

Yeah—all this without even having met this person. I don’t know how I fell for her. All I know is that I did.

How does this even happen? Am I a complete idiot?

Do I not understand that our online presence is a curated version of ourselves? That it does not fully represent who we really are? That whatever online interaction I had with her didn’t mean much?

What if she turned out to be a serial killer, or the one who learns “career hacks” from Hitler, or an unabashed fan of Tony Kakkar? What will I do then? Cry at Goa Beach?

I know. I know it very well. Yet, again, none of that knowledge helped. Logic failed me.

The sad part is…it didn’t go anywhere.

When I confessed on text—weeks after a brief flirtatious exchange she had initiated, the only time it happened—she responded graciously. She sent me a long thoughtful note to my stupidly long confession.

The thing is, she didn’t say a definitive “no”. She took two hours to say, “I don’t know”. But it took me two months to know “I don’t know” is a polite way to say “no”.

Which meant months of suffering. Was her unavailability due to genuine busyness or lack of interest? Her monosyllabic responses should have made it clear that she wasn’t interested in conversations, yet I persisted. Most of the time, I worried about coming across as creepy or pushy, even sending one text in two weeks made me think that way, and at other times, I wondered if I wasn’t doing enough.

I was so proud of the confidence I carry in my professional life, but I somehow lost it all as I chased after her. The advisory council demanded I never appear desperate, which meant I had to fake it, because for lord’s sake, at one point, I was desperate. I can confirm it is absolutely possible to miss someone even if you have never met.

As someone who prides himself on understanding the world, what could be more painful than not being able to understand?

So I clung to this strange and parasitic thing called hope.

Hope kept me going when my logical brain clearly saw no point. Hope overtook my analytical thinking, making me see possibilities. Hope amplified the faintest signs of potential interest and ignored significant signs of disinterest.

So when I lost hope, it felt like loss—to feel I had lost something even when I never had it.

It would have been easier if I could blame her for something, but I couldn’t. She did nothing—which is the crazy thing. Nothing happened.

I had to accept the painful truth that my madness couldn’t be reciprocated. We couldn’t waltz beneath a starlit sky or share a tub of popcorn at the cinema. It was all a figment of my imagination, my mind running wild.

To cope, I filled the pages of my diary, wrote poetry, played James Blunt and Gajendra Varma on repeat, and sought out art that made me feel seen. My inexperienced heart shattered, and it took immense strength to put the pieces back together. (It survived.)

To this day, I still don’t fully understand what it was. I’m unsure if my story qualifies as a sob story—I’ve heard tales of tragic love that render mine insignificant. So, what’s the big deal?

The point is, I no longer care. I’m done trying to intellectualise my emotions. I truly am.

Subjecting my experience to the opinions of others only invites the invalidation of my feelings. They say it’s mere infatuation, a reflection of “unrealistic expectations” fueled by mindless romcoms and plain immaturity.

Perhaps they’re right. But I genuinely don’t care.

I refuse to let anyone diminish the authenticity of my feelings. They belong to me alone, and I know they were as real as death and taxes. I have to live with the consequences, so I hold the authority to define what that was.

This taught me a life lesson that I didn’t learn any other way: there are limitations to our understanding. Some things are better accepted and experienced rather than dissected and rationalised.

The nerd in me had to let go of the constant desire for explanations and analysis, and simply allow things to be as they are. They are beyond logical comprehension—and that’s okay.

V. The Believer

As I sat down to write this essay, I realised the pointlessness of comparing the lives of single and coupled individuals, a predominantly heteronormative discourse in which I actively participated.

The truth is there are endless ways to structure a fulfilling life. There is no definitive right or wrong way. I can choose whether I want to be single and content or build a life with someone I truly connect with.

So screw those stupid studies claiming to know whether being single or coupled up will make you live longer or be happier. I don’t even get why those papers exist.

The cold, hard facts don’t mean much for people like me who see relationships as more than a matter of convenience involving splitting rent, doing housework, and running errands.

What matters is what I believe in. And those beliefs come from the stories I imagine living—not from what some social scientists have figured out.

For now, I want to believe in the story that love is powerful and has the potential to make life more meaningful.

After an exhausting year, I realised it’s all about taking chances and having faith. I can’t ever be certain of what the future holds. I can’t predict if I’m choosing the right person or if there’s someone better out there. I can’t guarantee eternal love or know where life will lead me. The fear is real, and it tempts me to rationalise avoiding relationships altogether.

But you know what? I’m making a choice—a choice to believe that finding love is worth it.

I’m still scared as hell of making mistakes and getting hurt. But I guess it’s part of the deal: I can’t possibly find love without opening myself up to the possibility of being hurt again.

For now, I am letting my guard down and letting things happen—as someone who has been theoretically philosophising life to minimise chaos, I can’t believe I am writing this. But here I am, trusting that if this gets somewhere, I can navigate this crazy journey with a partner by my side.

In the wise words of the Hot Priest from Fleabag, “Why would you believe in something awful when you could believe in something wonderful?”

For now, I choose to believe in something wonderful.

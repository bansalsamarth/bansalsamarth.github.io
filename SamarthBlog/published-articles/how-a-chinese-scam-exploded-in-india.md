*This story was originally published on [The Morning Context](https://themorningcontext.com/internet/how-a-chinese-scam-exploded-in-india) (paywalled).*

Dipesh Nagpal wasn’t expecting the call he received at 7 pm on 12 August 2020. A product manager at a Chinese company in Gurugram, Nagpal had finished his work-from-home shift and was sitting down to dinner with his parents in West Delhi’s Rajendra Nagar. The caller was Ankit Kapoor, one of his bosses, who ordered him to drive 30 km to Gurugram for an emergency meeting at a location unknown to him. 

Nagpal hadn’t attended a physical work meeting since March, when India went into its first coronavirus lockdown, let alone been called to work after office hours. He was a new hire at Linkyun Technology [](https://d.docs.live.net/1366521331fcc237/Desktop/ROW/Section%201.docx#_msocom_1)Pvt. Ltd, a payment aggregator that facilitated the business of Chinese companies in India. “I thought maybe they are meeting a client and need me for a technical brief,” he said. 

He started on his motorbike, following directions Kapoor sent him on the phone. The destination turned out to be an empty tract of land—all he could see was a police station at a distance. He called up Kapoor. “He told me to come right in,” says Nagpal. 

Inside Gurugram’s Golf Course police station, Kapoor stood in handcuffs. Also placed under arrest were two other top executives of Linkyun, Dhiraj Sarkar, the second Indian director, and Yan Hao, the Chinese business head. Just hours earlier, a team of the [Hyderabad Police had raided the offices and residences linked to the company](https://www.financialexpress.com/india-news/online-gaming-racket-busted-by-hyderabad-police-four-people-including-chinese-national-arrested/2054550/) after flying in from the southern city. The police were acting on complaints from two people who had lost Rs 97,000 and Rs 164,000, respectively, in an online gambling fraud. Their bank statements showed Linkyun as the recipient of the gambling money. The police told Nagpal his company was behind a gambling fraud ranging into tens of crores of rupees.

“I thought, ‘Oh my god, we were making a payment gateway. How did this happen?’” He looked at Kapoor for an answer. All he got was: “Yan Hao has got us into trouble.” 

The police let him go after a brief interrogation. The next day, the arrests were all over the news. Nagpal sent a message to the Beijing-based chief technology officer on the company’s WhatsApp group asking what was going on. “I got a blue tick, but no response.” Soon after, he says, the CTO removed all China-based employees, including herself, from the group. 

At a [press conference in Hyderabad on the same day](https://www.thehindu.com/news/cities/Hyderabad/chinese-national-3-others-working-for-online-gambling-company-held/article32348189.ece), the police commissioner said his officers were investigating [multiple reports of suicide related to an online gambling fraud](https://www.deccanchronicle.com/nation/crime/260820/shady-deals-transactions-linked-to-chinese-firms-being-probed-in-game.html) with roots in China. The Linkyun arrests set off a hunt for the Chinese control of Indian scams that stretched from gambling into loans, investments, e-commerce and dating. Indian investigators went on a spree registering cases, raiding premises, freezing bank accounts and arresting people. 

This wasn’t just about the suicides, however. 

Tensions have been rising between India and China ever since border clashes between the two in June 2020, following which the Indian government had banned 59 Chinese apps and restricted investments by Chinese entities in Indian companies. Over the next 12 months, the authorities jailed 14 Chinese suspects and 78 Indian accomplices in connection with cheating, extortion and money laundering, among other charges. But every Chinese person in their custody, including Yan Hao from Linkyun, has a similar story to tell. “It’s like they have been briefed. They say they are either 24 or 25 years old. It is their first or second job. They don’t know anything. They have no idea about their company’s operations or motives,” says Madan Kumar, the investigating officer in the gambling case, at his Hyderabad office. 

“They say they were only sent to India because they speak English. They were filling in for someone who was visiting their family in China, but they got stuck because of the pandemic,” the officer says, adding that the suspected scammers use the same words to defend themselves. “It runs like a script.” 

A year after the first raids, the Indian police admit they have only caught small players in a much larger system managed by people who operate either deeply underground or entirely overseas. Meanwhile, the scams are spreading faster than ever. As Madan put it, “The Chinese scams have reached the next level. It is too much.” 

***

By August 2020, Ravi Sisodiya, a small trader based in Mathura, 160 km from Gurugram, had lost so much money that he was thinking of ending his life. He had been at it since the first coronavirus lockdown began in March. It started with an ad on UC Browser inviting people to “make rupees 1000-2000 every day sitting at home”. 

The link took him to a page that touted the stories of other Indians who had got rich this way. There was a WhatsApp icon, which he clicked. It connected him to someone whose profile picture showed an attractive woman. He said hi; she replied with a download link for an app called Gooe Club.

Gooe Club [asked users to bet](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=w_66DJfzFnA) on one of three colours on the screen. They had 90 seconds to choose between red, green or violet, and to wager between 100 and 100,000 rupees. Once all bets were placed, the page froze for 30 seconds, before revealing the winning colour. If you won, it doubled your money. You could place a new bet every two minutes. 

Sisodiya was quickly hooked, and recruited his older brother, Girish, a lawyer. The app’s representatives added them to a Telegram group that purported to help players win the game. “Two people representing the company gave us suggestions,” says Sisodiya. “At first, they would guide us on how much to recharge our app account for and what colours to pick. Later, they even gave us some formulas for predicting the winning colour.” 

Girish started with a Rs 500 bet. “The 500 turned to 600. I felt good. 600 turned to 700. I felt even better. But then, all of that 700 went away, and I was very sad. After losing Rs 1,000, I stopped,” he says.

But Ravi kept playing. The Telegram admins suggested putting in larger bets to recover small losses. He increased his stakes to tens of thousands of rupees. “I had lost so much there was no other option but to carry on. I would win small amounts and then lose big time. It was addictive.” 

Over the following months, he drained his bank accounts and even pawned his wife’s jewellery. The app’s name kept changing, but users’ login credentials stayed the same, and so did the pattern: “Whenever we put in a big bet, the colour suggested to us didn’t show,” Sisodiya said. By August, he had lost Rs 10 lakh. “I was ruined.”

Across India, thousands of players were trapped in the same cycle of small wins and big losses. Even though gambling is outlawed in 25 of India’s 28 states, the proliferation of smartphones, cheap data and digital wallets made [online real-money gaming, from poker to fantasy cricket, wildly popular by 2020](https://www.livemint.com/news/india/online-gaming-industry-to-touch-2-8-billion-by-2022-in-india-report-11611055419121.html). Their craze grew during the pandemic, as people were locked down and losing jobs. The colour prediction apps went viral. But losing on them had nothing to do with bad luck. The companies that ran them manipulated the game at every step, mainly by guiding their users on what colour to choose and how much to bet. When the bets were small, the colour suggested to them often won. When the bets were big, the majority of the players lost. 

![](../assets/images/chinese-gambling-scam/collage-text-messages.png)

Investigating officer Madan played the game and joined the Telegram groups himself to figure out how the apps tricked their users. He believes the results were controlled by a “back end program. Say, 10,000 people bet on green. In the last 30 sec, someone makes violet win.” 

To pass on misleading messages to players like Sisodiya, the companies employed a chain of people they called “agents”, who were often recruited from the ranks of vloggers and social media influencers with ready-made audiences. Among them was [](https://d.docs.live.net/1366521331fcc237/Desktop/ROW/Section%201.docx#_msocom_4)YouTuber Sohil Naiem, a university student in Jammu and Kashmir.

In early 2020, Naiem began playing the colour prediction apps, which were taking off across his social networks. After losing several times, he realized that the apps were really a scam, and [posted a video to his channel saying as much](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Qxku0pEzJbE). Hours later, he got his first offer from a betting company. 

A representative of a colour prediction app called Coem emailed him from a personal address. They offered him Rs 20,000 to put up a three-minute video recanting his earlier post and telling his followers to start playing on Coem. 

The betting apps built a formidable network of trust through their early users and influencers. They used YouTube to pull in users and Telegram to exploit them. Every popular app boasted at least one official group and dozens of unofficial ones where their paid agents acted as admins and engaged the users.

After Naiem’s first interaction with Coem, another representative contacted him and offered him a chance to become an admin. His brief was clear: “I would get 40% of whatever losses I enabled.” (He deleted the emails from the company’s representatives after learning about the police investigation of the betting companies.) 

They said tips would come to him perfectly crafted in a private chat; all he had to do was convince the users to follow them. “Initially, if a company shares 10 tips with their users, seven will be right and three will be wrong. Once they have built trust, they reverse that ratio. That’s when you all start losing,” he says. 

“The company even had a strategy for a tie. Say, 50% of people bet on red and 50% bet on green. The display would show violet as the winner. In every scenario, the company profited.” 

Naiem went on to receive offers from representatives of multiple colour prediction apps over emails, WhatsApp chats and YouTube comments. But, even after months of interactions, he had neither any clue about their real identities nor any access to the companies they worked for. At the same time, he was receiving messages from friends and strangers alike who pleaded with him to help them recoup their losses. 

“My doubts were growing,” he says. “One day I received a message from one of them in Chinese language. It was deleted immediately, but I had already copied it and pasted it on Google Translate. The message was meant for a reporting manager. It noted the number of agents who had been recruited and included financial data for that week.” 

Eventually, after receiving a fresh offer from Coem, Naiem went for the kill. “I told the person who was communicating with me that there was only one condition on which I would work for you: someone from the company would have to speak to me over the phone.” 

Someone did call him afterwards claiming to be from the company. She told him it was headquartered in the US and registered to do business in India. “From her accent, I could tell she was not from India. I asked her where she was based, and she said Singapore. I could tell she was Chinese.” 

## A network in India?

In July 2020, Venkatesh Sivakumar, an Indian cybersecurity researcher, set out to prove the Chinese roots of the gambling scam. It started with a friend forwarding a text message offering him “the most popular way to make quick money”. He clicked on the link and landed on a cloned website of India Today, one of India’s biggest media houses. Its homepage showcased an article about people becoming rich overnight just by spending a few minutes on their phones every day. The illustrative visual featured Facebook (now Meta) CEO Mark Zuckerberg. 

Sivakumar knew he was looking at a fraud in the making. 

He soon found a whole web of similar domains and dug into them. The first 18 he investigated had links to China: their domains were registered either in Hong Kong or the mainland, the source code included comments in Mandarin and certain links in the Android app were connecting to servers in China. Some of the domains ran on a content management system built in China. 

The dots were connecting. One of those domains was gooe.in, which carried the link to download the app on which Sisodiya lost Rs 10 lakh. When he and other betters recharged their wallets on these apps, their bank transfers showed the money to be going to Linkyun, the Chinese-owned company whose directors would be arrested a month later. 

The following police investigations revealed gambling to be just one of many online scams run and enabled in India by Chinese entities. The victims are promised incredible things in exchange for money—hot dates, instant loans, e-commerce discounts, doubling investments—that were either never meant to be delivered or were hiding their intended price. 

This isn’t happening in India alone. Loan scams run by Chinese-owned companies have also been reported from Kenya, gambling scams from Malaysia and dating scams from Egypt and Pakistan. Much of this activity, particularly in predatory lending, followed a crackdown by the Chinese government that drove such operators out of the country. (Read: “[How a Chinese-run NBFC built a Rs 1,300 crore business in a year](https://themorningcontext.com/internet/how-a-chinese-run-nbfc-built-a-rs-1300-crore-business-in-a-year)” and “[Inside the scramble to cut off Chinese loan apps](https://themorningcontext.com/internet/inside-the-scramble-to-cut-off-chinese-loan-apps/)”.)

Fraudulent Chinese companies enter India on the tail of the tech giants such as ByteDance and Tencent, their eyes on the developing country’s massive low-income population—“the next billion users”. While the technical infrastructure powering the explosion of the online scams is built and maintained in China, the human resources are cross-border. The scams are executed through vast networks of Indian employees and agents who are either fully or partially in on the fraud. 

“Most Indian people working for these companies do not know what they are up to. Only one or two Indian employees do,” said Y. Srinivas, a cyber inspector in Hyderabad. “The masterminds are in China.” 

This is how the colour-prediction scam took off. 

***

In 2017, Vinod Kumar came to Delhi from a village in Uttar Pradesh to learn Mandarin at a private language institute. Chinese companies seemed keen to do business in India, and he had heard translators could charge them up to $100 for a day’s work. After finishing the course, Kumar went to Kunming in China for a year-long “improvers programme”, aimed at preparing students to work for Chinese companies overseas. In July 2019, he saw a job posting in a WhatsApp group of India-based Mandarin-English translators. He messaged the Chinese phone number mentioned in the job ad, using WeChat. The person on the other end was Shen Bo, who offered Kumar a job at [Sily Consulting](https://www.zaubacorp.com/company/SILY-CONSULTING-SERVICES-PRIVATE-LIMITED/U72900HR2019PTC082613), a Shanghai-based company that said it wanted to enter India with a portfolio of mobile games built around market trading. 

The job ad was for a translator, but what the company wanted Kumar to do was help set up its India operations, from scratch. He accepted the job at a monthly salary of Rs 80,000.

His first task was to register the company in India, and to do that, a chartered accountant pointed out to him, it would need an Indian director. The company offered him the position. Kumar, who was 23 at the time, refused. “I told them I am not at that level. I won’t be able to. I don’t have the capabilities,” he says, adding that he didn’t trust them. “I didn’t know what they were up to. I just wanted to get a salary from them.”

As a workaround, the chartered accountant got one of his friends to sign up as the company’s director on paper. The “dummy directors” act as the foundation stone for dubious foreign companies in India. Interviews with dozens of Indians listed as directors of shady Chinese companies revealed that many had no clue about their stated or hidden purpose. A trader signed up [](https://d.docs.live.net/1366521331fcc237/Desktop/ROW/Section%202.docx#_msocom_6)because his friend, a chartered accountant, asked him to; a grocery shop owner was offered Rs 5,000 every month to lend his credentials; and a blue-collar worker didn’t even know that he was named a director at a Chinese company.

“These companies engage Indian people as directors. They are paid Rs 5,000-6,000 every month. Bank accounts, agents, tele-callers are all India-based,” said Srinivas, the cyber inspector in Hyderabad. 

Sily Consulting found its own bunch of dummy directors. That done, Kumar got the company rolling. He rented an office in Gurugram’s Global Foyer Mall and hired a set of Indian employees. The India office ended up having 50 local employees who answered to five Chinese executives. The Indians brought in and dealt with the customers while a team in China handled tech. Closer to launching its product in India, the company flew Kumar to Shanghai to tour the headquarters and see the work in progress. “They wanted to test my Chinese speaking skills and see how well I could work in a Chinese environment,” he says. When Kumar was being shown around the Shanghai office, he said he saw the in-house employees work on a trading app.

Back in India, he continued to accompany the Chinese executives to their meetings with potential partners as a translator. His bosses often met representatives of Indian payment gateways such as Cashfree. They discussed user transactions, money flow and commissions. Sily executives never revealed the real nature or purpose of the product, says Kumar. “They claimed the company was into online shopping.”

But the shopping website was only meant to act as a front for a fraud. [](https://d.docs.live.net/1366521331fcc237/Desktop/ROW/Section%202.docx#_msocom_7)Kumar says he knew Sily’s intentions weren’t honest, but he did not imagine the company he helped set up would end up at the centre of a national police investigation. In August 2020, at the press conference following the arrest of Linkyun executives in Gurugram, the Hyderabad police [released a list of eight companies](https://www.hyderabadpolice.gov.in/assets/news/2020/08_August/13082020.pdf) they suspected to belong to the same web as the payment aggregator. The police claimed this web of Chinese companies ran a flourishing gambling scam. Sily Consulting was second on the list.

But the police did not have much information on Sily. Madan, the investigating officer, says they did not pursue investigations beyond Linkyun. Sily had closed their offices months before the Gurugram raids because of COVID-19. All they know about the company is that it had financial transactions with the payment aggregator under investigation. 

Our interviews with seven former employees point to a much bigger role. Five of them confirm that Sily ran a product called [Lefey Gold](https://www.mouthshut.com/review/Lefey-in-review-usuopmlnmno), a gambling app that later evolved into Gooe, the colour prediction platform where Sisodiya lost Rs 10 lakh. 

“It was very obvious that the company was running a scam,” says K, a Chinese translator hired by Sily as a business associate, who asked not to be named. At the time of hiring, K was told the company ran a gold trading app. That, she figured out within weeks, was a smokescreen. “Sily was not running a trading app. It was a gambling operation where they were fooling people.” 

When K joined in 2019, the company was running a betting scam based on gold trading. Colour prediction would follow later, but the basis of their earlier fraud was the same: misleading tips. Lefey Gold invited its users to start trading instantly. Users didn’t need to open a demat account or follow any other formalities: they could just sign up, recharge and get going. Hundreds of customers signed up every day. To earn a profit, they had to bet along the direction of the price of gold; they made money if their guess was right and lost otherwise. K’s job was to assist Sily’s customers in making those calls. Her mandate was to make them lose. 

K says Sily drew in customers by placing ads inside gaming apps and promised quick money. Clicking on the ads led people to a WhatsApp number handled by employees like her, and once connected, she introduced them to Lefey, shared download instructions, and set them on a betting spree.

Fraud was integral to the app. The gold prices on Lefey did not mirror the movements in the real commodity market. The company manipulated the numbers. “We told customers, ‘bet your money and gold price will automatically go up’. But when maximum people had bet on the price going higher, my boss would manipulate the price of gold to go down on the app,” K says. 

Her boss was a Chinese employee at the Gurugram office. “No Indian other than Vinod [Kumar] could even look at that machine,” K says. Two Sily employees accused him of being complicit in the fraud. Kumar denied their allegations. 

When the users felt they were defrauded, they took it out on the same people who had passed them the tips. It got ugly at times. “People would send us vulgar videos and hurl abuses when they lost money,” K says.

She left the company in two months. “I am not that kind of person who can work for a scam.” 

***

K is currently jobless, but her phone is flooded with “earn from home” messages sent by companies like Sily. The pandemic-induced economic crisis has ensured they have enough takers. 

Many of these scams are run from Gurugram, the go-to destination for Asian companies. The city provides a wide range of services to the incoming Chinese businesses, from restaurants and supermarkets selling home products to management consulting firms sorting out the logistics. 

Once they are registered in India, often the first thing Chinese scam companies do is hire an Indian employee to see them through the first few months. The first Indian hire is usually offered a lucrative salary and numerous perks. Their work is praised, and their opinion is sought. “They are treated as if they are God,” says a former Indian employee of a Chinese internet company, asking not to be named. But few Indian employees dare believe that their Chinese bosses trust them. The Indian workers say their Chinese managers never reveal their real names or phone numbers, leave alone their full intentions. 

All they are expected to do is play their part in the con. Most of the Indian employees we spoke to say they didn’t have access to the planning behind the scenes, whether by Chinese executives in India or by parent entities in China. But if they leave a job, they can easily find another. The companies don’t operate at the same scale, however. The scams are as likely to be run by five people from a dorm room as cross-border syndicates in corporate towers.

The Chinese translators job hunting in Gurugram straddle these disparate setups. “Numerous Chinese companies are running out of small flats in Udyog Vihar. Many are running betting operations. The companies are not registered, and their work is not legal,” says a former Sily staffer who followed multiple job ads after leaving the company. It is hard to know if the different Chinese-origin scams are orchestrated by organized networks or exist in isolation. Our reporting did throw up some links.

The office next to Sily’s on the sixth floor of Gurugram’s Global Foyer Mall belonged to a company called Groal Enterprises. Most of its employees (380 of 400) were tele-callers who worked as debt collection agents for more than 10 lending apps that gave out small-ticket loans between Rs 1,500 and Rs 15,000. Hundreds of such apps, mostly Chinese, launched on Indian mobile stores in 2020 and triggered multiple police complaints of fraud and harassment over the course of the year. In December 2020, following a nationwide crackdown on lending apps, Groal Enterprises shut down its office. 

The police teams separately investigating loan and gambling scams don’t know that Groal and Sily had common ownership. A former employee of Sily Consulting says he was hired on the letterhead of Groal. Sily’s first employee, Vinod Kumar, who attended top-level meetings, says Groal’s founder-director, Shen Bo, sold his company to the next-door neighbour. 

## Stopping the scams 

Oblivious to these links, the police are closing in on Linkyun. As of July 2021, the company’s top three executives remained jailed in Hyderabad while four police stations across the state of Telangana took turns to interrogate them. Across India, more than a dozen cases of gambling fraud have been filed against Linkyun to date, and the accused are also visited in jail by police teams flying in from other states. Special departments for economic offences are chasing the money trail into and out of Linkyun’s bank accounts. Delhi’s enforcement directorate even sent a team to Northeast India to find evidence in the home of Dhiraj Sarkar’s parents. “They took away a lot of papers,” says a close friend of Sarkar who lives nearby. But a year after the arrests, the Hyderabad police cannot prove their original claim that Linkyun masterminded the gambling fraud. What is not in question is their financial complicity. 

The police also hold them guilty of covering up the crime. Investigating officer Madan says the company’s India offices had received police summons about the suicides before the August 2020 raid, but all they did was cover their tracks. “In their company WhatsApp groups, the team used to discuss these summonses. Dhiraj, Ankit and other Indian employees used to bring up with their Chinese bosses the need to operate payment gateways under new names,” he says. 

The company continues to deny that charge. In China, Linkyun’s parent company, [Beijing Tomorrow Power](https://www.itppay.com/), defines itself as “a leading mobile Internet payment solution provider”. The company was established in 2010 in Beijing and has an office in Shenzhen. 

Its lawyers stress that Linkyun was “purely a third-party payment aggregator” in India and never ran any website or app “involved in gaming or gambling”. They claim its executives are in jail because of a “massive misunderstanding” on the police’s part. “If there is a crime committed by a payment gateway’s merchant, the payment gateway won’t necessarily have much to do with that,” one of them argues. 

According to them, Linkyun conducted standard checks before signing up new merchants but had no clue about their underground operations (fake websites, hidden apps, private Telegram links) or intentions. The police won’t swallow this claim. Linkyun may not be running a scam, but it would easily find out if any of its merchants was. As part of managing payments on their platforms, Linkyun often received the customers’ complaints, and many of them spoke of being cheated after having paid up on an app to find a date or a loan. “The customer care executive used to receive complaints about dating and betting apps. He had even written an email to the Chinese head in India before Yan Hao’s time suggesting that the company drop those merchants,” an employee says, requesting anonymity. 

However, the trial isn’t likely to begin anytime soon. Linkyun’s lawyers believe the Indian police is piling on FIRs to keep their Chinese client in jail for as long as possible. The police deny any agenda on their part. In the meantime, Linkyun has ceased India operations, Sily Consulting has vanished from the scene, and many new scams have emerged. They are more successful and better shrouded. 

***

As many as 106,000 people have seen [Fiewin’s](https://www.fiewin.com/#/Login) latest post on its official Telegram group, which has more than 300,000 subscribers. It announced a “winning streak period” and asked its users to “invite friends to participate”. 

Launched in October 2020, the gambling app has been adding thousands of new users every day in spite of the increased scrutiny.

Fiewin is only one of dozens of colour prediction apps to hit the Indian market since the August 2020 arrests. Since then, the earn-from-home scam has branched out in many new directions.

The newer apps don’t lurk inside fake media portals and shopping websites but target their user base directly through text-message spam. The messages remind the readers of the pandemic’s effect on jobs and invite them to click on a link to get rich. The links take them to apps, and to start earning, they are told to either complete a daily task or invest a daily sum. As with any other easy-money fraud, the early users are rewarded for a few weeks until everyone starts losing money to these apps.

Multiple users have filed police complaints in the past year, but the rate of the apps’ spread far exceeds that of the raids and arrests. 

“For every app, there must be thousands, even hundreds of thousands of victims,” says lead investigator Ashish Reddy at Hyderabad’s Economic Offences Wing, which recently busted an investment scam. But only a few register complaints. “That doesn’t mean that to the others, the loss doesn’t matter. These are people to whom every rupee matters. And for the company, because they operate at such volume, even one rupee defrauded per person adds up to a lot,” he says. Indian investigations typically halt after unearthing the web of companies and directors. 

The apps launched after last year’s crackdowns have new layers of secrecy that make it almost impossible to dig any deeper. 

Earlier, no one could reach the people behind the scams; now, not even their representatives leave a trace. They use a new personal email address even to contact the same person again and rarely make a phone call. Many new agents say they have only interacted with Telegram accounts. The platform allows users to send messages without revealing their phone numbers, a godsend for every scamster. 

## The new model

Fiewin [](https://d.docs.live.net/1366521331fcc237/Desktop/ROW/Section%204%20part%202.docx#_msocom_1)exemplifies the new model. Shortly after its launch, Souvik Choudhary, a 19-year-old college student in Maharashtra, signed up as an agent. “I came across a job ad that promised a commission of Rs 5,000-6,000 daily and provided a link to a Telegram channel for those interested in applying,” he says. He initiated a conversation even though he couldn’t see the name or phone number of the person on the other end. “I said I was interested in the job. They asked me if I was a student or professional and if I could work part time or full time. I said full time. They said I would have to put Rs 5,000 in my app account to start with.”

To attain more secrecy, the scam companies have created a new money flow between users and themselves. This features “recharge agents”. Choudhary was hired as one of them. His job was to collect the money from Fiewin’s users into his personal bank account so the company wouldn’t have to make deals with payment processors with legal-world imprints.

Choudhary was added to a Telegram group with 115 other recharge agents of Fiewin. If a user wanted to bet on a colour, they first went to Fiewin’s website and pressed the recharge button. That produced the details of the digital wallet of a recharge agent. They made a transfer to that wallet and uploaded the screenshot to the same website. “My job was to upload that amount to their app account after verifying the screenshot,” he says. The recharge amount ranged between Rs 20 and Rs 1,000. “Every day, I was supposed to do two rounds of recharge totalling Rs 20,000-30,000,” he says. Collected by 116 agents, Fiewin’s daily recharges would be over Rs 20 lakh. 

If anyone were to trace the flow of the users’ money, they wouldn’t be able to go beyond the bank accounts of the recharge agents. This practically insulates the new companies from being identified in the case of an event such as the Linkyun arrests.

The secrecy continues further down the process. The company behind Fiewin collects the gambling haul from its recharge agents in cryptocurrency. “If I received Rs 10,000 in recharges, I would have to buy cryptocurrency worth that amount on the Binance app and send that to my boss’s wallet on that app,” says Choudhary. Crypto wallet addresses are a long string of letters and digits with no personally identifying information.

This system also saved the companies from user complaints. “I used to get calls from pan India. From every state, from every city,” he says. Towards the end of his two-month gig, he became “suspicious of the company’s intentions. Something was fishy. Users were calling all day complaining they were not able to withdraw their earnings.”

Choudhary had no answers for them. “We [recharge agents] were beginning to get frustrated with all the secrecy. We didn’t know a thing about the company we were working for. Where was it based? What was its name? Sometimes, our reporting manager accidentally sent us messages in Chinese. She would delete them immediately after, and send the same message in English. We never spoke on the phone. No voice messages either. All we knew is that people we were communicating with on Telegram did not know Hindi.” 

Fiewin fired Choudhary before he could bring himself to quit. Since then, he says some of his friends have tried to recruit him to similar companies they work for, but he is done being an app agent. 

Meanwhile, the piles of FIRs are growing at police stations across India. Sitting behind one in his Hyderabad office, Ashish Reddy says these cross-border online frauds will keep striking in newer forms as long as it finds in India millions of people willing to bet their lives for easy money. “The crime never stops, it just transforms.”

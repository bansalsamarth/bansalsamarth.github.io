The Existential Afterlife of My June Feelings

by: Samarth Bansal | 12 June, 2024

It’s been ages since I’ve rambled on the internet and (over)shared my feelings. No one asked for them, no one’s waiting with bated breath. I know. (Reminder: readers aren’t lovers.)

But I can’t help it. My narcissistic side takes feelings so seriously that it has this existential need to give them an afterlife beyond my inner life. And seriously, why not?

Let’s get meta. I’ll start by revealing my feelings on feelings themselves.

Context first. In the last six weeks, I’ve upended my entire life. If you’d told me six months ago that I’d leave Landour for Mumbai, take a leadership role at a fast-growing startup, and build my dream magazine, I would’ve laughed in disbelief.

I worshiped the slow life. I wasn’t exoactly judgy, but I did feel that a peaceful life in a small town, being close to nature, taking on less work and enjoying more leisure (thanks, my privilege) was the answer to isolating oneself from the darker side of capitalism that has turned us humans into always-on, always-producing machines. It goes against human nature and becomes a cause of perpetual dissatisfaction. One must see through that farce, peel down the invisible layers, shatter illusions. The more people do this, the more they will realize the futility of how the modern world is constructed.

And then—in what may seem like an extremely stupid move years from now—I just reversed all of it. I walked into exactly the lifestyle I felt was the reason modernity was in such chaos.

Why did I do it?

I could give you a long-winded answer featuring logical-sounding career moves and personal growth, but the truth is this: I felt like doing it, so I went ahead and did it. These stupid little things called feelings, which the world doesn’t want me to take seriously, led me to change my whole world.

The weird thing is, while this time has felt so significant for me in how I experience the world, it also feels—and this is objectively true—so insignificant in the larger scheme of things. I mean… does the fact that water stopped coming out of my water filter this morning, a day after I got the plumbing fixed in my newly rented apartment because the washing machine outlet was not connected to the sewage, really mean anything when a genocide is happening in Gaza? Have I lost my mind?

Forget the world. Things are so different even compared to what people close to me are going through.

As I sent WhatsApp voice notes to friends about my Hinge experience being so good in Bombay (more on that later), two of my married friends were going through divorce proceedings.

As I am so excited about imbuing all the work I do with meaning, a friend—super talented, which I am not sure he often recognizes—is trying to find a job by gaming the corporate system, speaking their language to get in, even if it involves deception.

As I am consciously moving to a big city, eager to build a new life, a writer I once edited revealed to me he had to move cities out of circumstance—a choice he would not ideally make.

There is so much suffering around me that I can’t help but wonder about the hidden worlds behind everyone’s faces. What is it that we don’t know about how others experience the world? In the absence of that knowledge, how are we supposed to behave when it’s only my own existence that I have full access to?

This is why I really care about my feelings, even if you, my dear reader, may not. It’s my way of acknowledging the ultimate existential reality: we are all fundamentally alone in our tiny little universes. No one else can ever fully understand the subjective experience of being someone else, an outcome of perceptions, emotions, and thoughts. Of our values, fears and aspirations.

So yeah, bigger things might be happening in the world, and my problems might be objectively small—from how to find excellent young writers for my new magazine or raise money, to how to stay on a high-protein, calorie-deficit diet for my sub-15% body fat goals, to setting up my house and finding my cafes and the right woman to date—but they are my problems. Finding a way through them is what gives my life meaning. Much of this everyday stuff will stay invisible, but in dealing with it, you make space for the visible things, which matter beyond just the self.

That’s how things are, and that’s okay. The story of my life—the one I am experiencing—will feel like the world to me. It will feel high-stakes because this is the only reality I can fully inhabit.

And that’s even more reason to tell our stories and express our feelings. How else will we find out what’s behind those happy faces? How else will we discover the common ground, that we are not alone in feeling alone? It effectively leads to a paradox: we are alone, yet our lives are enriched in the presence of others.

Does it make sense to you? My feelings are asking: do you see what they want to say?

This is a perfect point to segue into what I have lately been thinking a lot about: kindness.

In some ways, it’s a no-brainer: the world would be so much better if more of us were kinder. But that’s not how the world works.

Let me just talk about myself. Increasingly, among all the virtuous things a ‘good’ human would aspire to, kindness has become my North Star. When you are stuck in a situation and don’t know what to do, the morally right thing is to be kind. And it’s not that hard.

It really matters. Hear from the always amazing George Saunders: 

So here’s something I know to be true, although it’s a little corny, and I don’t quite know what to do with it:

What I regret most in my life are failures of kindness.

Those moments when another human being was there, in front of me, suffering, and I responded . . . sensibly. Reservedly. Mildly.

Or, to look at it from the other end of the telescope: Who, in your life, do you remember most fondly, with the most undeniable feelings of warmth?

Those who were kindest to you, I bet.

It’s a little facile, maybe, and certainly hard to implement, but I’d say, as a goal in life, you could do worse than: Try to be kinder.

…

Do all the other things, the ambitious things — travel, get rich, get famous, innovate, lead, fall in love, make and lose fortunes, swim naked in wild jungle rivers (after first having it tested for monkey poop) – but as you do, to the extent that you can, err in the direction of kindness. Do those things that incline you toward the big questions, and avoid the things that would reduce you and make you trivial. 

The words spoke to me so deeply that I am most likely getting this framed and putting it on my wall.

And on that note, one more related thought: be kind to the world, yes—but be kind to yourself, too. This is something I keep reminding myself in the pages of my diary when I am doing self-therapy.

I don’t want to get into the details of the creative anxiety I am dealing with at the moment (it’s normal, and there’s no point sharing it until I create the thing I have set out to create), but it really hits you.

So just sharing one bit I wrote in my diary on the day I announced The Plank, the magazine I am now building:

“If you ever feel broken, remember: you took action. To make a small change. To make things (slightly) better. It takes courage to follow your conviction and trust your creative instincts. So be proud of yourself. 

But… never let this make you feel any inflated sense of self-importance. Never. That’s the trap. You are doing this because you want to do it—there is no moral imperative.”

That’s a note to self: Work very hard. But don’t forget to be kind to yourself.

It’s already 8:30pm on this Wednesday evening and I want to wrap up this blog. So the last thing I will talk about is the thing I have so much to say about… dating.

I am fully aware that whatever I am going to say next may sound super weird, but what can I do if my feelings are just that… weird. (So please be… kind?)

As a single man in his thirties — just 30, but same — and in a city, I am in the dating market. And when you are there, it’s normal to be on the apps.

The puzzling thing is, as a man with borderline-average looks (which is what matters most on these algo-matchmaking bits of code), my baseline assumption is that I am supposed to make an account, doom scroll like a zombie, get one or two matches in a day or two, and then try to strike up a conversation and be fake-interesting to make the woman, who has too many matches to care, talk to me. Then see if it leads to a date. It’s totally insane, but we are so habituated to it that it’s just normalized.

But only the algo lords know what the hell is happening in Bombay: I just keep getting matches. And it’s not just likes that lead to matches when men like me try way too hard with the first message. Likes are coming from the other end. I will sit, and my phone will buzz: “X has liked you. Match to continue chat.”

That’s not it. Women in Bombay are also… talking? They initiate conversations and reply instantly? They follow up?

Okay, make no mistake: I am not dissing women. It’s just all of us being weirdos on the apps. I’m simply narrating my common experience of these broken conversations, which apparently has changed for me in the new city. And I don’t understand why.

Good for me, I guess?

At first, this increased attention was exciting. More matches would mean more conversations, which would mean a whole new world of possibilities. 

But this unfamiliar territory revealed something else: as these matches have started coming at this frequency, and now I have so many open chats to respond to, I don’t feel like talking to anyone. Because while earlier this process felt off due to a lack of choice, now it feels off because of more choice. What are we really doing here? And why?

I mean… part of the reason I sound so jaded is that I went on one date that didn’t go well. I am not into bitching about bad dates on the internet, but if you have ever been on one where right in the middle both people know they should just go their separate ways ASAP but are still kind of stuck together for some reason, that’s what happened to me. Thanks, Hinge.

As more matches keep coming in, I am finding myself less and less inclined to engage.

The even weirder part is that just the act of getting likes is inducing this super odd feeling. It’s not about wanting to actually connect with these matches or go out with them. It’s more about the fleeting validation that comes from knowing someone—anyone—found you attractive enough to swipe right. (It might potentially be mindless right swipe, but whatever.)

It’s like a momentary ego boost, a quick dopamine hit that confirms there’s something desirable about you, even if it’s just based on a superficial first impression. And somehow, that’s enough. That tiny dose of attention and approval feels good, even if it doesn’t lead anywhere meaningful.

I hate to admit it, but maybe the apps are fulfilling a need I didn’t even know I had: the need for external validation by the opposite sex. And now that I’m getting it in doses I’ve never experienced before, it’s messing with my head. I thought I was above seeking validation from strangers on the internet, but apparently not. (Sigh.)

It’s a strange realization, to see how easily we can be influenced by these little digital gestures of approval. They tap into some deep-seated human desire to feel wanted, to feel attractive, to feel like we matter. Even if it’s all just a game.

And then the spiral continues and I wonder: who really wants to date like this and go on these soul-sucking dates through these soul-sucking apps?

Whoever it might be, it’s not me. 

Whoever it might be, it’s not me. What many of us secretly want—or at least what I want—is to fall stupidly, recklessly in love. To experience that all-consuming passion, even if it means accepting the inevitable pain that comes with it. The inside jokes and the shared vulnerabilities…and yes, even the heartbreak.

Because here’s the thing: heartbreak sucks. It’s messy and devastating and it can leave you feeling like a shell of yourself. But in a twisted way, it’s also a testament to the depth of your feelings, to the fact that you cared so much, you dared to love so fully.

It’s been what… just two and a half months since the last brutal heartbreak? And here I am, craving that intensity again, wanting to jump into this aag ka dariya, that sea of fire, knowing full well it might burn me? Whatever it is—better than whatever is happening on the apps—it’s because …feelings. Which defy all logic. 

Chalo. Enough. Bye.
